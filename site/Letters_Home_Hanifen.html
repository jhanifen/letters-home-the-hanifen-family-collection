Foreword

The Hanifen family collection tells the story of three brothers — Bob (Robert), Jim (James Jr.), and John — from Des Moines, Iowa, as they navigate the upheavals of World War II, the Korean War, and the slow return to civilian life in postwar America. At the center is Bob Hanifen, whose letters span from his first weeks of military training in 1944–1945 through his post-WWII occupation duty in Italy, his discharge and resettlement in Dallas, Texas, and his ongoing correspondence with family back in Iowa well into the 1970s. The collection opens on a family already embedded in American working-class life: James Hanifen Sr. runs Hanifen Body & Paint Co. at 904 Keo Way in Des Moines, and by the summer of 1944 all three sons are swept into military service by the demands of WWII.

Bob's wartime letters form the richest vein of the collection. From basic training at Sheppard Field, Texas and the University of Wyoming's ASTP program, through technical training at Chanute Field, Illinois and then deployment to Italy in early 1946, Bob's letters to his father are a vivid, unfiltered record of a young Midwestern man encountering the wider world. He writes about B-29s and carbine rifles, about Italian women and German POWs, about the thrill of his first cross-country flight and the agony of wisdom teeth pulled without anesthesia. His voice is unmistakably his own — colloquial, affectionate, occasionally crude, always honest — and it never quite loses its wonder at the strangeness of military life abroad.

The Korean War chapter, told primarily through letters to and about John Hanifen, adds a darker register. John serves with the 439th Engineer Construction Battalion, a unit that performed extraordinary feats of military engineering — including what was officially recognized as the highest railroad bridge repair in the history of military engineering. He is at times in real danger (Communist guerrilla attacks near Pusan), subject to military discipline (a court martial reduces his grade in mid-1952), and dependent on letters from home and Dallas for connection to family life. Bob writes to him from Dallas with a mixture of brotherly teasing, genuine concern, and practical encouragement — urging him to make Sergeant, to use the GI Bill, to come home for Christmas.

Underlying all the military correspondence is the family anchor: James Hanifen Sr., the father, who writes in pencil on body shop stationery about elk hunting, pheasant season, Republican conventions, steel strikes, and the slow business of winter. His letters are brief, folksy, and deeply affectionate, and they root the entire collection in the texture of postwar Des Moines. By the 1950s and 1960s, the brothers are dispersed — Bob in Dallas, John rotating through Korea, Jim apparently back in Iowa — and the letters increasingly carry the weight of separation: Christmas hopes, debt repayments, photographs of children the uncles haven't met. The 1976 letter from Aunt Pauline, with its hair loss and calculators and dead relatives and newborn grandchildren, closes the arc quietly: the family has survived war and dispersal, and is now simply aging.

Chapter 1
Before the Storm: Home Front and Early Military Life (1941–1944)
1941 – mid-1944

This chapter encompasses the Hanifen family's pre-deployment years and the earliest phase of Bob's military service. The documentary record here is sparse but telling: James Hanifen Sr.'s 1941 driver's license and the WWII gasoline ration book establish the family's grounded Des Moines existence — a working-class auto body shop owner, a 1941 Plymouth, wartime rationing. Bob graduates from high school around June 1944 (Letter 19 is a thank-you note for graduation gifts) and is already contemplating college or the Navy Air Corps. His brother Jim Jr. is stationed at Fort Riley, Kansas, in the Cook and Baker School. The family is intact but about to scatter.

Letter 16, a July 1944 note from Dad to Jim Jr., captures the father's anxious pragmatism as Bob enters the ASTP program at the University of Wyoming — receiving no pay, needing soap and towels sent from home, on a grueling 6 AM to 10:30 PM schedule. The University of Wyoming letters (Letters 7, 18a, 65, 66, 49, 75) show Bob in a military-academic hybrid existence: room inspections, obstacle courses, basketball games, typhoid shots, and a girl who promises chicken dinner twenty miles out. The ASTP experience is both formative and temporary; when it winds down, Bob will flow into the standard AAF training pipeline.

This is the largest and most kinetic chapter of Bob's wartime experience, documented through dozens of letters from Sheppard Field, Texas, Chanute Field, Illinois, Fort Leavenworth, Kansas, and Greensboro, North Carolina. Bob cycles through basic training, weapons qualification (Marksman with carbine and .45), gas chamber exercises, bivouac, and endless reassignments as the AAF struggles to match training pipelines to the war's shifting needs. His frustration at being reshuffled from teletype operator to sheet metal worker to 'Air Corps unassigned' mirrors the broader chaos of mid-1945 mobilization. Throughout, his letters to his father are warm and detailed — the food, the weather, the buddies, the girls — and deeply revealing of a young man maturing under pressure.

The most historically charged moment in the entire collection arrives in Letters 28 and 45: Bob writes on August 9–10, 1945 from Chanute Field referencing 'this new bomb' and then the next day reports that 'the Japs want to surrender.' He is almost certainly writing about the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki, and his response is characteristically practical — he hopes it gets him home faster, but worries he will be assigned two years in 'the Islands' as occupation army. The war ends with Bob still at Chanute Field, finishing a sheet metal course that graduates September 22nd, meaning he will miss his birthday, Thanksgiving, and Christmas at home.

Sorry I haven't written sooner but we just got back from bivouac and out there we never had any time to write. We fired the 45 cal. pistol on Mon. for practice and Tues. we fired for scores. I made Marksman and it wasn't bad considering about 30 never qualified and only [about?] a few made Expert.

We had so little time we never washed in three days. Boy what a life. We walked constantly from difference [different] lectures on all kinds of warfare. Chapter 3
Post-War Italy: Occupation, Observation, and Coming Home (January – Late 1946)
1946-01 – 1946-09

After processing at Greensboro and Camp Kilmer, Bob ships out to Naples in January 1946 aboard the SS NYU Victory (Letter 84), arriving February 19. The Italy letters (Letters 3, 4, 6, 8, 11, 15, 18, 20, 22, 23, 24, 25, 39, 73, 80) form the richest sustained portrait of Bob abroad. Stationed first near Naples and Caserta, then traveling to Foggia, Bari, and Manfredonia for inspections, Bob serves as an ordnance inspector in a motorized unit — a job that suits him well and earns him praise from his lieutenant. He sends home detailed, unfiltered observations of post-war Italy: bomb damage, German POW labor, the barter economy of cigarettes, Italian women, the Red Cross, displaced persons fleeing Yugoslavia, a riot in Naples that kills fourteen people. He is simultaneously a tourist and an occupier.

The Italy letters also show Bob's financial responsibility: he sends home almost all his pay in money orders, building up war bonds and savings for his return. His requests home are modest — 620 film, swimming trunks, the sports pages from the Drake Relays — and his affection for his father ('Lots of Love Your Son, Bobby') is unwavering. Jim Jr.'s February 1946 letter from Bethany, Missouri (Letter 2) adds a parallel thread: Jim is also processing out of service, emotionally raw after a car incident he blames himself for, in a letter that captures postwar disorientation in miniature. By mid-1946, Bob appears to be winding down his Italy posting and anticipating his return to the United States, though the precise discharge date is not documented in the collection.

Feb. 21, 46

Dear Dad,

Arrived in Naples the 20th at 1200 eat chow on ship and left at 1300 the 7 Service Depot. We came up in trucks 50 to a truck. Big open air job. Right through the heart of the city. Those Air Corps and Infantry men really worked this place over. Blew hell out of it. Most of it is cleaned up now. It wasn't hurt as bad as the papers in the states said so. The Degas [Dagos?] took and thumbed their noses [at the] [?] sign and smiled all at the same time. What filthy bastards. One girl sounded off and said look at the American son-of-a-bitch. Swear[s] a hard time. We pulled into a big area filled up with big buildings, palm trees, swimming pools, tennis courts, roller skating rinks, stadium and really a beautiful place. Statues all over. What was funny tho was all the holes in the buildings. It was formerly a German Headquarters. It was Mussolini's Naples Expedition [Expeditionary?] way — round, supposed to represent Africa. It is really nice. We live in small wooded barracks with a big building in the Patio [sketch of barracks layout] as nice barracks deal. Good food. PWs do all the work on the field. All we do is pulled guard. Pretty rough for a lot of Italians has been going in camp by 19 and 20 [hundred hours]. All I think [know?] about to tell they say. Sound like war at night around here. Am in the Red Cross building now. Beautiful. Wish I had a camera to show it to you in picture. It took us 10 days to get here. We stopped a day at Casablanca. What a town. Filthy Arabs. Beg for shit damn near. We food away from the sea hull [?] if they can. At least in better shape than Naples but it really took a pounding. 3 mph [?] we are lying in shallow water holes in them like your [?]. Big liners ran aground to the left of them. One boat is sunk so only the mast is still showing. Those Arabs are really the deal — the native cops beat hell out of them with the back [of] jack [?]. All the time for getting near the boat. They say in Naples here, they will sell you for anything. Had a [robbery?] last month. Beat hell and stole everything but his shorts. If you get a pass to town you are allowed to carry your own choice of weapon. .45 Carbine, M1 or whatever you get your hands on. This American Dego [Dago] are in like Flynn over here. All of them no Dego. The women are sluts all of them. As one time for two cigarettes. Cigarettes all for 2325 lire a carton [equals] $2 dollars in American money to anyone. Down town 36 or 40 bucks. But that is black market. I can send home all my money, which is mid [?] over the Army pay table. Not will be with my insurance and one band [?] out about 46 bucks. You can live damn good on your cigarettes ration. Just got back from the show. Doll Face and the Spanish Main. Beautiful theater, about the size of the Shrine [theater] here. Huge place. I will enclose this map of our voyage till we probably ship in 48 hrs. from now. A lot left of barracks already. Well Dad goodbye for now.

Your loving son Bobby

Recognized as one of the most outstanding Active Reserve units in the Corps of Engineers in [Fifth?] Army area, the 439th Engineer Construction Battalion (VRC) was one of the first of such units to be called to active duty by the Department of Army for service in the Korean conflict. Affiliated with the Kansas Contractors Association, the unit was called to the colors on [14?] August 1950. With Headquarters at McPherson, A Company at Emporia, B Company at Hays, and C Company at Salina, Kansas, the unit had an initial strength of 28 officers and 215 enlisted men. Most of the key positions were held by World War II veterans, both officers and men, widely experienced in heavy construction through association with one of the larger contracting firms in Kansas.

Arriving at Camp Carson, Colorado, on 26 August 1950, for assignment under the 32d Engineer Construction Group, this battalion and four others spent four tedious months trying to conduct unit training constantly thrown off schedule by irregular arrival of filler personnel, most of whom arrived only two weeks before the units departed for overseas, by an almost complete lack of T/O&E equipment, and by administrative and POM requirements.

Upon its departure for FECOM on 8 January 1951, the battalion collected a large share of its construction equipment at the POE for the first time. After a three-week stop over in Japan during which time additional equipment was procured, the unit finally arrived in PUSAN, KOREA, on 8 February 1951.

Finding itself in a bleak, desolate, and mountainous combat zone relatively untrained, untried, and yet to see most of its big equipment for the first time, the members of the battalion nevertheless approached their assigned tasks eagerly and aggressively and were destined to show that this battalion would live up to the expectations of the Department of Army that, through its Organized Reserve Corps program, an Active Reserve unit, particularly in the Corps of Engineers, could readily adapt itself to arduous field service in an acute emergency.

Almost immediately after its arrival in PUSAN harbor, members of the unit worked assiduously day and night in adverse weather and under the trying dockside bedlam, to off-load its equipment. After a brief period of staging outside PUSAN, the 439th Engineer Construction Battalion became one of the first units to be operational under the 32d Engineer Construction Group with responsibility for reconstructing and improving, initially between ANDONG and SEOUL, the badly damaged railroad, and the completely inadequate MSR between ANDONG and TANYANG, both being vital supply routes directly supporting X Corps and attached units fighting then in the vicinity of WONJU.

In spite of extremely hazardous roads, inclement weather, and constant threats from guerrilla attacks along the MSR, this unit disregarded the advice, subsequently proved erroneous, of personnel in higher headquarters in Japan and EUSAK that it was foolhardy and virtually impossible to move large engineer equipment forward from PUSAN on the MSR, and proceeded to move its equipment forward in one of the most outstand-

[continued page 2]

ing achievements of the kind in Korea. The battalion at this time, February-March 1951, although battle lines were in turmoil, now found itself deep in X Corps area frequently ahead of elements of combat engineer units and in the close proximity of division CP's. Combat engineer commanders working slightly ahead on the MSR were astounded to see such quantities of large and vitally needed equipment in Corps but alone being so far forward in the Corps area.

The extraordinary manner in which the Battalion organized itself after landing in Korea and the determination, aggressive spirit, and outstanding ability of its personnel quickly set it apart and above other units with similar missions.

With the vital ANDONG Railroad line, the only railroad in eastern and central Korea connecting PUSAN with SEOUL through ULSAN, YONGCHON, CHECHON, and WONJU, in a serious state of repair, many of its destroyed bridges being expeditiously but unsafely repaired by Koreans, several tunnels either blocked or severely damaged, and major river crossings being made on temporary low-level structures threatened by floods at any time, rail traffic supporting the central front in March 1951 was very uncertain and could proceed with caution only as far north as CHECHON. With the front pushing ahead slowly approximately 30 to 40 miles to the north, opening of the WONJU railhead 20 miles to the north was of supreme importance to the tactical situation. In the meantime all supplies had to be moved forward by truck from CHECHON over an unimproved, rough and time-consuming road. The opening of the railroad to WONJU would mean the location and rapid expansion of supply dumps closer to the front, would permit evacuation of wounded by rail nearer to the front, and would eliminate truck transportation over 35 miles of narrow, rough road including a 7-mile one-way mountain pass. In effect supplies would be 4 hours closer to the front when the railroad could again operate into WONJU.

Accordingly, the most vital of all Group missions was given to the 439th Engineer Construction Battalion to open the railroad to WONJU in three weeks. To do this, three major tasks presented themselves; first, the reconstruction of the KIRA-CHON Bridge, the highest railroad bridge of its kind in Korea spanning a deep valley to connect two tunnels on either side; second, the clearing of a tunnel blocked completely by the explosion of an ammunition train in the tunnel itself; and third, the rebuilding of the WONJU railyard lying in complete shambles just six miles north of the KIRA-CHON Bridge.

With the CCF threatening attack in April, the key to the herculean task of rapidly opening the railroad to WONJU was obviously the rapid reconstruction of the KIRA-CHON Bridge. This structure consists of ten spans, total length of 800 feet, its steel girders being supported by steel towers near the center of the bridge and concrete piers near the abutments. During the retreat of UN forces earlier in the war, two 103-foot high steel towers at the center of the bridge were destroyed by demolitions removing a total of 270 feet of steel girders and track.

The plan of attack in rebuilding this vital structure consisted of [continued]

(a) remove debris of damaged towers, (b) erect new towers to facilitate erection of new towers by cranes still standing, (c) erect two new towers and transfer thereof portions of two other towers on still standing, (d) fabricate one continuous new girder 270 feet long, weighing 135 tons, from 40" I-beams, (e) launch the continuous beam on rollers across gaps between each tower, and (f) [jack?] the beam down into position.

During the period 1-22 April 1951, utilizing two construction companies of the battalion supplemented by equipment and personnel from other units in the Corps, the unit rendered one of the most outstanding engineering achievements in Korea, officially recognized as being the highest railroad bridge repair in the history of military engineering, and became the first Military Engineers ever to launch a continuous beam of the record length of 270 feet weighing 135 tons.

This unprecedented achievement was carried on under hazardous working conditions in cold, wet weather, subjecting the men working on steel over 100 feet in the air to the most severe tests. In spite of seemingly insurmountable problems, the members of the battalion by exemplary application of construction principles, through extraordinary devotion to duty working under severe pressure for long hours, completed this unparalleled engineering project in an exceptionally meritorious manner and in time to permit supplies to enter WONJU by rail during the crucial stages of front line action. It is significant that this work was completed without a fatality or serious injury in spite of the inexperience of the troops on high-steel. In recognition of this unsurpassed military engineering achievement, six Bronze Star Medals were awarded to individual members of the command.

While the KIRA-CHON Bridge was being repaired, work was also being rushed on a tunnel which had been completely blocked and badly damaged by the explosion of an ammunition train within the tunnel just north of CHECHON. Exposed to the dangers of burned ammunition, severely cracked concrete tunnel lining and jagged debris of the train, members of the unit removed the wreckage and debris only after many hours of back-breaking and arduous work only to have the roof cave in completely at one point to such an extent that the ground above soared into the tunnel and blocked it again. Enlarging the hole which formed above ground was continued to prevent further sliding of material into the tunnel while emergency repairs were rapidly undertaken in the tunnel below. Although not as spectacular as the KIRA-CHON Bridge project, this work was equally as important and necessary in opening the railroad to WONJU as was the bridge repair; the temporary repairs were completed in an exceptionally meritorious manner under extremely hazardous conditions by the time the bridge was completed on 22 April 1951.

The third important step in opening the railroad from CHECHON to WONJU involved the complete reconstruction of the WONJU railroad yards completely destroyed by bombing and the explosions of ammunition trains. From what had once been an original trackage of 10,000 feet, of which 9,000 feet were destroyed, a new rail-head was constructed giving usable trackage of 22,000 feet. Storage and unloading areas were increased from 12 acres to 140 acres, and service roads were increased from 1300 feet to 26,000 feet. In addition loading ramps, electrical lighting systems and improved water-supply systems were installed. The bulk of this work was done between the latter part of April and June 1951. Because of being heavily committed on the KIRA-CHON Bridge and on tunnel repair, another unit completed preliminary work of clearing the yard of wreckage, initially begun by the 439th Engineer Construction Battalion, and the laying of track for partial operation of the yard. Immediately after rail traffic was reopened to WONJU the latter part of April 1951, the unit took over all work in the railhead and developed it to the dimensions enumerated above.

As soon as the 439th Engineer Construction Battalion took over this work after it had so successfully completed its mission of reopening the railroad to WONJU, various service units immediately moved forward from CHECHON and clamored for depot areas and spur tracks for off-loading supplies. With the realhead and surrounding areas in the process of being graded, with streams of trucks arriving constantly for supplies and ammunition from the front, and with the rainy season approaching, it became essential that all-weather storage areas, spur tracks, and access roads be prepared all the more rapidly. Concentrating most of the battalion on this new crisis, members of the battalion worked unceasingly to keep up with the ever increasing demands. By their exceptional devotion to duty, tireless effort, and outstandingly efficient operations the members of the battalion constructed what is now recognized as a model railhead and certainly the most outstanding in south central Korea. As a result of this well-planned, completely gravelled and well-drained facility, the speed in unloading trains and handling of supplies was so increased over what had previously existed or was thought possible in other railheads that this effect alone on the logistical support for X Corps was of inestimable value in the combat efficiency of forward units.

While concentrating on the urgent work in the WONJU railhead, orders were received by the battalion to expedite its efforts in the opening of the railroad between WONJU and SEOUL, a distance of 68 miles. This portion of the railroad had to be reopened without delay since it was parallel to the rear boundaries of both I and IX Corps along the HAN River and, in view of the poor road net in those areas, would increase immensely the delivery of supplies to units deployed immediately north of the railroad and assist in expediting a build-up for defense against an expected attack by the CCF down the PUKHAN River valley.

Working from WONJU westwardly and from SEOUL eastwardly toward the PUKHAN River, the Battalion was faced with the formidable task of reconstructing 15 railroad bridges and relaying approximately 150,000 feet of rails and 70,000 ties destroyed by UN forces in previous withdrawals. Upon the collapse of the 6th ROKA Division in early May, the work was completely interrupted and units of the Battalion were forced to withdraw rapidly to WONJU. Immediately after the tactical situation was stabilized, the battalion resumed its work from WONJU toward the PUKHAN River,

the portion from SEOUL to the PUKHAN River being assigned to another Group in the meantime by the Army Engineer.

In the space of thirty-five days, 15 April-20 May 1951, two major bridges west of WONJU were reconstructed and the track repaired opening the railroad 25 miles from WONJU to CHIPYONG-NI immediately behind the 7th US Division. By 2 June 1951, work was completed on three more bridges and the track was rebuilt to open the railroad to LANGTONG [YANGPYONG?], 35 miles out of WONJU, immediately behind the revitalized 6th ROKA Division. By 17 July 1951, the track and three bridges west of LANGTONG [YANGPYONG?] were reconstructed making the railroad operational to the PUKHAN River Bridge, 48 miles from WONJU. In this prodigious effort between WONJU and the PUKHAN River, the battalion had reconstructed 10 concrete piers, utilizing native contractors and had transported and launched a total of 14 girders in spans previously demolished in eight major bridges; nearly 48 miles of track had been completely rebuilt as well as rehabilitation of three small railyards at CHIPYONG-NI, YONGMUN, and YANGPYONG.

The exceptionally meritorious accomplishment of opening the railroad first from CHECHON to WONJU, and then immediately beyond to the PUKHAN River, contributed immeasurably to the logistical support and combat efficiency of tactical units during the critical CCF spring offensives. The battalion completed this mission under the most adverse conditions of weather and enemy activity. Although not trained or even equipped for railroad construction work, the unit--through the diligence, devotion to duty, and exceptional efficiency of its members--rendered an outstanding measure of support to tactical units by expediting the flow of supplies forward by rail to supply points which could now be considerably closer to the front. The many problems engendered by inadequate supplies of construction materials and equipment, inaccessability of bridges and rail, inexperience of troops, working under pressure of deadlines, were faced with resourcefulness, skill and determination by the 439th Engineer Construction Battalion.

The terrain in the zone of responsibility of the 32d Engineer Construction Group and assigned units in east central Korea was much more rugged than on the western side of the peninsula due to the SOEBEAK [SAEBEAK?] range and major spurs extending therefrom generally westwardly across routes of communication, chiefly the SOEBEAK [SAEBEAK?] range. As a result, the units had more mountain passes to contend with on the MSR and laterals than did units working along the western side of Korea.

The first major pass encountered, as units progressed northerly in the early phases of operation in February and March 1951, was the incomparable TANYANG PASS, the severest bottleneck on the X Corps MSR between PUSAN and WONJU. Extending a distance of 16 miles between PUNGGI and TANYANG, were 12 miles of dangerous narrow, crooked, mountainous bench road the highest and longest one-way mountain pass in central Korea on an MSR. At the bottom of many ravines, and at the foot of the vertical cliffs and high banks around which the narrow and treacherous road was originally built by the Japanese, were many wrecked ve-

hicles of all types which had plunged from the over-dangerous road. With barely enough room for one vehicle, disaster had occurred repeatedly when vehicles tried to pass one another or when vehicles had skidded on the ice and snow during winter months. It was not unusual for convoys to spend from 2 to 4 hours to cross the pass including time waiting for traffic.

The 439th Engineer Construction Battalion was the first unit of the Group to tackle TANYANG Pass. Due to the short time it remained in the Pass in March 1951, prior to proceeding north on aforementioned railroad work, the unit was engaged in 24-hour operations of keeping traffic open and blasting away some of the many critical points. Hampered by snow, ice, and mud this unit was solely responsible for moving the large volume of traffic over the pass during the critical fighting immediately to the north. Working without any let-up, in blizzards, driving sleet and extremely low temperatures, and displaying superior devotion to duty, vitally needed supply convoys proceeding to CHECHON and beyond were able to get through the pass in spite of the treacherous road and frequent dangerous land slides which blocked the route several times during the day and night. Elements of the 10th NKPA Division then recently cut-off in the TANYANG area frequently harassed troops working in the pass compounding the extremely dangerous and hazardous conditions already existing. The 439th Engineer Construction Battalion displayed such indomitable courage and aggressiveness in the face of seemingly insurmountable difficulties during this early period in its operation as to win the respect and admiration of all units it assisted.

Being fully committed on critical railroad work during the next few months, the Battalion's next major project on the MSR did not occur until in June and July 1951. Between CHECHON and WONJU, approximately six miles south of WONJU, another relatively short but extremely treacherous defile existed being famous for its short-radius reverse curves through and around a rocky promontory and deep gorge. The battalion undertook the construction of a completely new section of road to eliminate this defile. This required a deep cut through rock of some 35 feet for a distance of nearly 1000 feet, followed immediately by a fill of 50,000 cubic yards at a height of 30 feet across the mouth of a narrow gorge. Completion of this project provided a two-way, class 50, roadway eliminating delays to traffic of from 1 to 4 hours which previously occurred on the X Corps MSR between CHECHON and WONJU. The notable feature of this project was the unprecedented and bold method employed in completely eliminating the defile rather than attempt to improve the existing road. Beyond the capabilities of many engineer units, the 439th Engineer Construction Battalion immediately recognized the challenge presented and with incomparable aggressiveness, exceptional efficiency, and remarkable resourcefulness, completed the project in an outstandingly superior manner between 1 June and 23 July 1951.

In subsequent shifts of responsibility to the north of WONJU in late summer 1951, the Battalion quickly eliminated extreme curves in the five-mile pass south of HONGCHON and then devoted much of its effort in the pass just south of HANGYE on the road from HONGCHON toINJE.

This latter pass, known as HANGYE PASS, consisted of a one-way, four-mile, northbound route and a similar length two-way southbound route containing grades as high as 16 percent. Obviously, with cold weather approaching, a very serious interruption of traffic would occur if traffic were precluded from using the southbound route due to icing conditions, and were forced to use the one-way northbound route of lesser grades but subject to dangerous slides and complicated by many hair-pin turns.

Accordingly, the battalion in the late fall undertook development of HANGYE PASS northbound route into a two-way, class 50 highway. Here again it was necessary to construct radical new alignments in several places to provide the desired results. A fill of a record height of over 135 feet was constructed in one location to eliminate a one-way horse-shoe curve benched around a steep cliff high above the HONGCHON River. Displaying the ultimate in aggressiveness, professional skill, and applying construction principles gained from many years of experience, the members of the battalion completed this urgently needed road reconstruction in time to eliminate the danger of complete traffic breakdown in freezing weather.

PROPOSED CITATION FOR MERITORIOUS UNIT COMMENDATION

The 439th ENGINEER CONSTRUCTION BATTALION is cited for exceptionally meritorious conduct in the performance of outstanding services in Korea in support of combat operations during the period 8 February to 31 December 1951. Responsible for reconstructing, improving, and maintaining the severely damaged routes of supply both railway and highway supporting X Corps and attached units, the 439th ENGINEER CONSTRUCTION BATTALION accomplished its vital missions under the most adverse condition of weather and enemy activity and in spite of critical shortages of materials and supplies. Through the diligence, professional ability, and exceptional devotion to duty of its members, the battalion's unprecedented reconstruction of the highest steel railroad bridge in the history of military engineering at KIRA-CHON, Korea, was an extraordinary achievement permitting timely and greatly increased support to both IX and X Corps by opening the railroad to WONJU during the 1951 spring offensive of the enemy. The final chapter is slender but resonant. The collection's latest item is Aunt Pauline's 1976 letter (Letter 9) to Bob — a chatty, affectionate note about hair loss from bone-degeneration medication, a sold car, the Barquist grandchildren turning 16 and 24, Ann's overdue second baby, and Marcus's ruined fishing nets. It is a letter from old age — warm, slightly bewildered, full of small losses and small mercies — and it closes the collection with the same familial intimacy that opened it thirty-five years earlier.

Between the 1954 estate documents and the 1976 letter, the record is thin. But the arc is clear: the Hanifen brothers have survived two wars, dispersal across the country, financial struggles, and the ordinary attritions of middle age. Bob, the collection's central figure, has moved from a Des Moines kid writing home from Sheppard Field about gas mask drills to a middle-aged man in Dallas receiving keepsakes from an elderly aunt. The family's story is, in the end, a quintessentially American one — working-class Iowans scattered by history, held together by letters.

She worked at United States Fidelity and Guaranty Co. in Dallas under claims superintendent Mr. Chittenden. Her November 1951 typed letter to John is warm, humorous, and socially engaged — she offers to set John up with girlfriends, teases Bob for opening her mail to John, and shares family news including Jim's scheme to manage the shop. She appears to have been recently married to Bob at the time of writing (signs herself 'Your sister, almost').
Pauline F. Hanifen (Pauline, Aunt P.F. Hanifen, Aunt Pauline, P.F. Hanifen)
Bob's aunt; family correspondent; keeper of family keepsakes
Pauline F. Hanifen is Bob's aunt (probably his father's sister or sister-in-law). She writes to Bob in 1976 with a collection of 'treasures' (family keepsakes), reporting on her health troubles (bone degeneration medication causing severe hair loss), family birthdays, Ann's overdue baby, Marcus's ruined fishing nets, and various family phone calls. She is warm, self-deprecating about her handwriting, and clearly close to Bob's extended family. She is also mentioned in the Warnock estate Table of Heirship as 'Pauline Miller' — suggesting her birth name may be Pauline Miller, connecting her to the Miller family that is co-heirs of the Warnock estate.
Pearl Miller (P. Miller)
Family member or close family associate; mentioned in 1976 letter
Pearl Miller is mentioned by Aunt Pauline in Letter 9 as a separate correspondent whose 'scribble' is better than Pauline's handwriting. Given the Warnock estate Table of Heirship (Letter 17) which lists 'Peal Miller' as an heir of Ollie Miller alongside Pauline Miller, Pearl Miller appears to be a sibling or close relative of Pauline, and thus part of the Miller branch of the extended Hanifen family.
Milo Olivier (Milo)
Army buddy of John Hanifen; 443 Engineer Pipeline Company
Pfc Milo Olivier (US 550 678 92) of the 443 Engineer Pipeline Company at Fort Leonard Wood, Missouri, writes to John Hanifen in October 1951. He is crude, candid, and genuinely fond of John — frustrated with Army life and envious of friends who have been discharged. He got John's address from John's father during a delay en route home. His unit was originally slated to go to France as part of NATO buildup but the deployment was cancelled.
Lt. Davidson (the Lt., the Lieutenant)
Bob Hanifen's superior officer in Italy; former pilot; Ordnance Inspector's office
Lt. Davidson appears repeatedly in Bob's Italy letters as his direct superior — a 'swell guy' who lets Bob do inspections independently, goes on vacation to Switzerland, lets Bob drive the jeep, and praises Bob's inspection reports. He is a former pilot reassigned to ordnance inspection work, and his fair, collegial treatment of Bob is clearly appreciated. He is one of the positive figures in Bob's wartime experience.
M. C. Miller (Lt. Colonel) (Lt. Col. M.C. Miller, Commanding Officer, 439th Engineer Construction Battalion)
Commanding officer of John Hanifen's Korean War unit
Lt. Colonel M. C. Miller, Corps of Engineers, commanded the 439th Engineer Construction Battalion during its 1951 Korean tour. His appreciation letter to John Hanifen is formal but genuinely admiring, documenting the battalion's extraordinary accomplishments in railroad bridge reconstruction, railroad rehabilitation, and mountain road improvement. He specifically credits John with contributions to the Han River girder launches and the Som River highway bridge — Korea's longest.
Jack Buckley (Jack Buckley)
Friend/connection of Bob Hanifen in Italy; helped Bob get a camera and arranged outings
Jack Buckley is a recurring figure in Bob's Italy letters — a friend or connection who helps Bob get a camera, takes him and his lieutenant to wrestling matches in Foggia, and serves as a social connector in the post-war Italian posting. He is described as a 'sweet guy.' Bob visits him in Foggia on inspection trips.
Alvin Miller (Alvin)
Family friend in Laramie, Wyoming; fishing companion
Alvin Miller (return address 808 S. 2nd, Laramie, Wyoming) and his wife Carolyn are close family friends of the Hanifens. They correspond with Jim Hanifen Sr. and Jr., relay messages from Jimmie overseas, invite the family to visit for fishing, and by August 1945 have purchased a house in Laramie. Alvin appears in Letter 18a as someone Bob meets while at the University of Wyoming. Given the Warnock estate Table of Heirship (Letter 17) listing 'Alvin Miller' as an heir of Ollie Miller, Alvin is almost certainly a member of the Miller branch of the extended Hanifen family.
Edith (Edith)
Family member; possibly Jim Sr.'s wife (Bob's mother) or a close family associate; deceased by 1976
Edith is mentioned repeatedly across the collection but never fully identified. She is mentioned in Letter 18a as a recipient of greetings ('Tell Fran & Pauline hello & John too'), in Letter 31a as the recipient of warm regards from Alvin and Carolyn Miller ('How is Edith?'), in Letter 61 (Bob buys her a present), and in Letter 9 (1976) where Aunt Pauline notes that 'young Jimmy called when Edith died.' The Miller family's warm regard for her and Jim Jr.'s centrality in Letter 31a suggest she may be Jim Jr.'s wife, but the pattern of Bob buying her a present and the family's collective references to her also support her being the mother/wife of James Sr. Her death, mourned in the 1976 letter, is a significant family loss.
